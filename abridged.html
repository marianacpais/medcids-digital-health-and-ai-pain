The pains of pain data

A single numeric pain score captures almost nothing about the patient's actual experience.

What the patient said
“It's worst at night, so I barely sleep. I had to give up gardening last year. Some days I'm fine for hours, then it flares and I'm scared to leave the house. I keep thinking the surgery made it worse — nobody really listens to that.”
Missed by the score: temporal pattern, location, function, catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, emotional context, the patient's own explanatory model.

Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROs) in chronic pain

PROs.Information reported directly by patients about their health, symptoms, quality of life, and treatment experience — without clinician interpretation.

Patient voice

Pain is inherently subjective — no biomarker captures it fully. PROs are the only direct window into the patient's experience.

Clinical outcome

Function, intensity and impact captured longitudinally — a basis for monitoring response and shared decision-making.

Regulatory relevance

Increasingly required by regulators and payers as a measure of treatment effect in chronic conditions.

Wearables and digital biomarkers in pain

Digital biomarkers.Objective, quantitative, physiological or behavioural data collected by digital devices.

Physical activity

Accelerometry as a proxy for functional capacity.

Sleep quality

A critical pain modulator; bidirectional with intensity.

Heart rate variability

An indicator of autonomic nervous system state.

Skin conductance

Arousal and stress responses, linked to flares.

GPS / mobility

Social and functional participation outside the home.

Smartphone behaviour

Typing speed and screen use as mood / cognitive signals.

Caveat.None of these measure pain directly. They measure signals that correlate with pain or its consequences.

The OMOP Common Data Model

OMOP.Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership — now stewarded by OHDSI ("Odyssey").

"To improve health by empowering a community to collaboratively generate evidence that promotes better health decisions and better care."

Optimised for population-level research, drug safety, and comparative effectiveness — not for clinical care.

Patient-centric individual records, not aggregates
Domain-oriented conditions, drugs, procedures, measurements
Vocabulary-embedded SNOMED · RxNorm · LOINC · ICD
Provenance preserved source codes kept alongside standard
Tech-neutral SQL Server · Postgres · Oracle

Why OMOP exists — origins and mission

OMOP began as a public–private partnership to study product safety using observational health databases.

In 2014, its assets were transferred to OHDSI — Observational Health Data Sciences and Informatics ("Odyssey") — a global research community.

Mission To improve health by empowering a community to collaboratively generate the evidence that promotes better health decisions and better care.

Design philosophy

The single design choice that explains the rest

Optimised for population-level research, drug-safety surveillance, and comparative effectiveness — not for delivering care or processing claims.

This is why ETL is hard: you're translating operational artefacts into analytical ones.

The European Health Data Space

EHDS · the regulatory backbone for primary and secondary use of health data in Europe.

Where it began

Ursula von der Leyen's mission letter (1 December 2019), addressed to Stella Kyriakides, Commissioner for Health and Food Safety.

Mandate: create a European Health Data Space to drive better care and reduce inequalities.

Goal: enable the exchange of health data and support new preventive strategies, treatments, and medicines.

EHDS sits inside a wider EU data strategy

Nine sectoral data spaces sharing a common technical and governance scaffolding — health is one of them.

The EHDS regulation

Proposed by the Commission in May 2022. Adopted by the European Parliament in April 2024.
COM(2022) 197 final — proposal, Strasbourg 3.5.2022
European Commission press release · 24 April 2024

Primary and secondary use

Timely and simplified exchange of, and access to, health data for different use cases.

EHDS for secondary use — how it works

  • Common European rules. Who must make which data available, for what purposes, and under what conditions.
  • Common infrastructure. A shared platform for data exchange across Member States.
  • Health Data Access Bodies. National orchestrators of the access process.
  • Data permits. Standard requirements with common safeguards before any reuse.
  • Data catalogues. Listings of the datasets that are actually available, where, and at what quality.

Data categories under secondary use

What can be made available — defined in Article 51 of the regulation.

Allowed and prohibited purposes

What you may and may not do with EHDS data — Articles 53 & 54.

Opt-out

Individuals have the right to opt out of the secondary use of their health data through EHDS.

It can be exercised at any time, without justification, and is reversible. The mechanism must be easy to access and understand.

Member States may set narrow rules so that, in selected cases and under strict conditions, opt-out data can still be made available for matters of public interest.

Left open by the legislator Where and how to exercise it (flexibility for Member States); the mechanism for making data available; temporal effect — opt-out applies only forward, not to existing licences and extractions.

EHDS within the EU regulatory framework

EHDS doesn't sit alone — it overlaps with GDPR, the Data Governance Act, the AI Act, MDR, and several others.

EHDS — overall timeline for application

Article 105: a phased rollout from 2024 through 2036, by use case and data category.

FAIR data principles

A framework for maximising the value of research data.
F
Findable
Unique identifiers; metadata that is indexed and searchable.
Pain example: a CRPS registry indexed in an EU dataset catalogue, discoverable by other researchers.
A
Accessible
Retrievable via standard protocols; access conditions are clear, even if data is restricted.
Pain example: opioid-prescribing data behind a documented EHDS data permit.
I
Interoperable
Standard vocabularies and formats — ICD, SNOMED, FHIR, OMOP.
Pain example: NRS scores stored as LOINC codes so they aggregate across hospitals.
R
Reusable
Clear provenance, licence, and quality documentation.
Pain example: a fibromyalgia cohort released with versioned phenotype definitions and a CC-BY licence.
Why it matters for pain research. Pain registries that aren't FAIR are harder to reuse and federate; FAIR is important for AI training; and EU funders increasingly look for it.

Federated analysis — research without moving data

Patient data can't leave the institution. So you send the analysis to the data, not the data to the analysis.
01
Each site converts its data to OMOP CDM locally.
02
Researcher develops an analysis package (OHDSI ATLAS or R).
03
Package runs at each site, on local data — no records leave.
04
Only aggregated, anonymous results return to the researcher.

Live networks. EHDEN (Europe) · DARWIN-EU (EMA) · the OHDSI international network.

Pain application A multi-centre opioid-safety study across 15 EU countries — without anyone sharing patient records.

Data governance and patient consent

Key concepts

  • Data ownership. Patient? Hospital? State?
  • Consent models. Explicit (opt-in) vs broad consent vs dynamic consent.
  • Purpose limitation. Use only for consented purposes (GDPR Art. 5).
  • Data minimisation. Collect only what is necessary.
  • DPIA. Required for large-scale health-data processing.

For pain research specifically

  • Chronic pain patients often have vulnerable profiles — consent must be genuinely informed.
  • Secondary use of pain-clinic data requires ethical approval.
  • EHDS introduces a supervised access model with data permits.

What it knows is bounded

A model can only know what was in its training data — and only up to the cutoff date.

Recent guidelines, local protocols, and post-training literature are invisible unless explicitly retrieved.

Bias is baked in

Training data carries the biases of its sources — under-represented populations, historical inequities, geographic skew.

For pain in particular, this matters: pain is reported and measured very differently across groups.

We can't always explain it

Even the people who build these models can't fully account for any single output.

Explainability tools help — but in clinical settings, this opacity is part of the regulatory and ethical conversation.

Why this matters now

Health information needs and clinical data are growing faster than humans can manage them — that's the gap AI is being asked to fill.

The AI Act

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — the first global legal framework for AI.

Key objectives

  • Ensure AI systems are safe and respect EU fundamental rights and values.
  • Promote investment and innovation in AI through a clear legal framework.
  • Enable lawful, safe and reliable AI applications in the EU single market.

Why we need it

  • Most systems are low-risk, but some create harms that need addressing to avoid undesirable outcomes.
  • AI decisions can be hard to understand — and may produce unfair disadvantages (e.g. hiring).
  • Pre-existing protection was insufficient for AI-specific challenges.

What is an "AI system"?

"An AI system is a machine-based system designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy, that may exhibit adaptiveness after deployment, and that — for explicit or implicit objectives — infers, from the input it receives, how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations or decisions that can influence physical or virtual environments."

💡 This definition was the subject of intense debate during the legislative process.

AI Act — who's in scope?

It applies to:

  • Providers placing AI systems on the EU market or putting them into service.
  • Users of AI systems within the Union.
  • Providers and users in third countries — when the output is used in the EU.

AI Act — risk levels

Obligations scale with risk.
Adapted from europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2021/698792

Unacceptable risk

Tier 1

Prohibited

Systems considered a threat to individuals — manipulation of vulnerable groups, social scoring, certain biometric identification and categorisation.

Real-time and remote biometric identification (e.g. live facial recognition) is banned, except under strict law-enforcement conditions with prior judicial approval.

High risk

Tier 2

Strict obligations

Not banned, but subject to strict obligations before market placement: conformity assessment, post-market monitoring.

Examples: critical infrastructure, education and employment, healthcare and product safety, essential services, remote biometrics, law enforcement, migration and justice.

Limited (transparency) risk

Tier 3

Information & transparency

Obligation to inform humans when interacting with an AI system (e.g. chatbots).

Generative AI providers must ensure outputs are identifiable and clearly labelled (e.g. deepfakes, public-facing informational text).

Minimal or no risk

Tier 4

No specific rules

The AI Act introduces no rules for AI considered minimal or no risk.

The vast majority of systems currently used in the EU sit in this category.

Examples: AI in video games, spam filters.

General-purpose AI (GPAI)

  • Specific rules for GPAI — applies to general-purpose models and those representing systemic risks.
  • Transparency obligations: technical documentation, downstream-provider information, copyright, text-and-data mining (TDM), training-content summary, EU representative.
Systemic risk GPAI with "high-impact capabilities" — providers must notify the Commission when training compute exceeds 10²⁵ FLOPs. Above that threshold, systemic risk is presumed. Additional duties: risk assessment and mitigation, cybersecurity, incident reporting, corrective measures.

Timeline & AI literacy

From 2 February 2025, organisations on the EU market must ensure staff working with AI have adequate AI literacy — applies to providers and users. softwareimprovementgroup.com/eu-ai-act-summary/
